Act 1 — The Trigger

A near-miss forces the question nobody had asked

A 28-person B2B SaaS company, three years old and scaling fast, ran its entire operation out of Notion: sales pipeline, product roadmap, client contracts, internal ops. The workspace had grown the way most do, one page and one shortcut at a time, with no one ever assigned to own the structure.

The trigger came when a new hire, added to a shared teamspace during onboarding, stumbled across contract terms from a different client. Nothing malicious happened. But the COO realized nobody could say with confidence who had access to what, or why. She booked a Notion Ops Audit: 90 minutes, $197.

Act 2 — The Audit Findings

What a structured review surfaced across all 7 domains

  • 14 guest accounts still active from projects closed 6+ months earlier
  • 3 teamspaces set to "everyone can edit," with no documented reason
  • No offboarding step to remove departed contractors' access
  • One 40-plus-property "Master Tracker" database nobody fully understood
  • No approval step before anyone could spin up a new database

None of this was a single dramatic failure. It was a workspace that had accumulated small, reasonable-at-the-time decisions until the sum of them became a real exposure.

Act 3 — Remediation

Turning findings into a governed system

The audit fee was credited toward a follow-on Workspace Design & Governance engagement. Over three weeks:

  • Access rebuilt around least-privilege; guest accounts pruned from 14 to 2
  • The Master Tracker split into 3 purpose-built databases with role-specific views
  • An offboarding checklist added directly to the HR onboarding template
  • A lightweight approval step added before any new database goes live
  • 12 unused paid seats identified and removed at the next billing cycle
Act 4 — The Transformation

Where the company stood 90 days later

New-hire onboarding dropped from two weeks to three days, since role-based views meant new employees saw only what their role required from day one. Zero unresolved permission flags remained, down from the 14 guest accounts and 3 open teamspaces found at audit. The COO could answer "who has access to what" with a single search, instead of a guessing exercise across a dozen pages.

When a prospective customer's security team sent over a vendor questionnaire, for the first time the company answered it in one sitting instead of scrambling for a week.

What changed, in numbers

Cost saved

12 unused paid seats removed at the next billing cycle, plus the admin hours previously spent manually tracking who still needed access.
