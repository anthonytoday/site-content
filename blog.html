AI Agents

Notion's AI Agent: what it actually changes for your workspace

Notion's AI agent can create tasks, populate databases, and run multi-step actions inside your workspace on your instruction. That is a meaningful shift from "AI that writes text" to "AI that touches your data." Before you turn it loose, ask a simpler question first: does the agent have access to anything it should not?

Most workspaces I audit have permission structures that were never designed with an autonomous agent in mind. Shared databases, guest access left open from a project that ended months ago, teamspaces where "everyone can edit" was the fast setup choice. An AI agent inherits the access of the account running it. If that account can see your finance tracker or a client's confidential notes, so can the agent, and so can whatever automation you build on top of it.

The fix is not to avoid the feature. It is to review access before you expand what touches your data: who can see what, which integrations are connected, and whether guest permissions still make sense. That review is exactly what a Notion Ops Audit covers, and it takes 90 minutes.

Automation

Custom agents in Notion: where they earn their keep, and where they do not

Custom agents let you configure a specific automation, say, one that files new leads, drafts a project brief, or updates status across linked databases, so it runs without you touching it each time. The appeal is obvious: less manual upkeep, fewer forgotten updates.

The part people skip is scope. A custom agent should have exactly the access it needs to do its one job, nothing more. I have seen agents built with broad database permissions because it was faster to set up that way, not because the task required it. That is a small convenience today and a real exposure later, especially once the agent touches anything with client or financial data.

When I build custom agents for clients, the first step is always mapping what the agent needs to touch, then locking everything else out. It takes an extra ten minutes at setup and saves a much longer conversation later.

Product Updates

What Notion 3.0 and the AI agent rollout mean for how you should be working

Notion's recent product direction, AI agents, expanded database permissions, and deeper AI integration across the workspace, signals a shift from Notion as a place you organize information to Notion as a place that acts on it. That is useful. It is also a reason to revisit your permission model, not assume the old one still fits.

Database permissions in particular deserve a second look. Notion has been giving teams more granular control over who can see and edit specific views and properties. Most workspaces I look at are still running on permissions set up two or three product versions ago, before that granularity existed. Update the feature set without updating the structure underneath it, and you get more capability sitting on the same loose foundation.

Staying current with what Notion ships is part of the job. Knowing which of those changes actually matters for your workspace, and which ones are just interesting, is the part worth paying for.

AI Agents

Three Notion AI features worth using this week

Three features stand out as genuinely useful rather than novelty: AI-generated meeting notes, the AI agent for task and database actions, and AI-assisted fields inside databases that summarize or classify rows automatically.

Meeting notes are the one I use in every setup session. Recording, summarizing, and filing a session automatically saves the client from a manual write-up and gives them a clean record to reference later. It also means nothing gets lost between "we discussed it" and "it's documented."

The database AI fields are worth a specific caution: if a field is set to summarize or extract from content the AI has access to, check that the source content is something you are comfortable an automated process reading and repeating. Sensitive fields should stay outside the reach of AI summarization until you have reviewed what that summarization actually surfaces.

Used deliberately, these three features cut real admin time. Used without a second thought, they can quietly expose more than intended.

Project Management

Running projects in Notion without it turning into another inbox

The most common reason people book a setup session is a project system that started clean and slowly became a dumping ground. Every new project gets a new page with a slightly different structure, statuses drift out of sync, and nobody fully trusts the tracker anymore.

A project system that holds up needs three things: one database, not five, with every project as a row; a small, fixed set of statuses that map to real decisions (not "in progress" meaning six different things depending on who wrote it); and views built for specific people or checkpoints, not one crowded master view everyone squints at.

The other piece people underestimate is ownership. Every project row should have exactly one accountable owner. Shared ownership in a tracker usually means no real ownership at all.

Get the structure right once, and the system stays useful for years instead of needing a rebuild every few months.

Automation

Keep project visibility clean with a few well-chosen properties

Visibility problems in Notion are rarely a database design issue. They are usually a properties issue: too many manual fields that someone has to remember to update, so half of them go stale within a month.

The properties that matter for visibility are the ones that can auto-fill: status changes that trigger a linked date, a rollup that pulls the true completion percentage from sub-tasks instead of relying on someone typing a number, a formula that flags a project as overdue based on the due date rather than a manual tag. Every property you can automate is one less thing that silently goes wrong when someone is busy.

Audit your current tracker with one question: which fields update themselves, and which ones depend on someone remembering? The ratio tells you how much of your "project visibility" is real and how much is wishful thinking.

Productivity Systems

Time blocking in Notion: the method, not just the calendar

Time blocking works because it forces a decision before the day starts, not during it. The mistake I see most often is treating it as a scheduling exercise rather than a prioritization one: people block their calendar around meetings that already exist, then wonder why nothing changed.

The version that actually works starts the other way. Decide the two or three outcomes that matter for the day first, block time for those before anything else goes on the calendar, then fit meetings and admin around them. Notion Calendar's two-way sync makes this workable in practice, since blocks and meetings live in the same view instead of two systems you have to reconcile manually.

The habit that makes it stick is a five-minute review at the end of each day: what got blocked, what actually happened, and why the gap exists. Without that review, time blocking quietly turns back into a to-do list with extra steps.

Design

Notion's layout options are a small feature with a large effect on adoption

Database layouts, board, list, gallery, calendar, timeline, are an easy feature to overlook because they change nothing about the underlying data. What they change is whether people actually use what you built.

A dense list view is efficient for someone managing the whole database. It is intimidating for a team member who just needs to see their three open tasks. Give that same data a filtered board view scoped to their name, and adoption goes up without a single property changing. The data was never the problem. The presentation was.

When I hand off a workspace to a client, I build at least one view per user type, not one view for everyone. It costs a few extra minutes at setup and it is the single biggest lever for whether a system gets used six months later or quietly abandoned.

Design

What makes a Notion page look considered instead of thrown together

The difference between a Notion page that looks professional and one that looks like a draft usually comes down to three habits: consistent heading hierarchy, deliberate use of callouts, and restraint.

Heading hierarchy sounds basic, but most pages I review mix heading sizes inconsistently, so the visual structure does not match the actual structure of the content. Fix that first, everything else reads better immediately. Callouts should mark the two or three things on a page that genuinely need attention, not every paragraph. A page where everything is highlighted highlights nothing.

Restraint is the harder one. The instinct is to add more, more color, more icons, more sections, when a page feels unfinished. Usually the fix is the opposite: fewer elements, more white space, and a clearer path through the content from top to bottom.

This is also exactly what makes template previews look premium, which matters if you plan to sell what you build.

Notion Sites

Running your website on Notion Sites: what I learned building this one

This site runs on Notion Sites, and the biggest lesson from building it is that the platform is genuinely capable for a small-business or solo-practice site, provided you treat content structure with the same discipline you would use for a client workspace.

The customization options have expanded enough that you can build a real multi-page site with consistent branding, not just a single public page. What still takes deliberate work is everything a visitor never sees directly: page load behavior, how content updates propagate, and keeping a consistent design system across pages that were built at different times. Those are workspace architecture problems wearing a website's clothing.

If you are a small business or solo operator considering Notion for your site, the platform is ready. The part worth getting right from day one is the underlying structure, since that is what determines whether updating the site later is a five-minute edit or a rebuild.
